The Conservation of Matter

What is the Law of Conservation of Matter?

The Law of Conservation of Matter states that the amount of matter stays the same, even when matter changes form. In other words, matter cannot be created or destroyed; it can only change from one form to another.

What is Matter?

Matter is anything that has weight (mass) and takes up space (volume). Everything you can see and touch is made of matter. The air you breathe, the water you drink, and the food you eat are all examples of matter.

The Three States of Matter

Matter exists in three main states:

  • Solid: Has a definite shape and volume (like a rock or ice cube)
  • Liquid: Has a definite volume but takes the shape of its container (like water or juice)
  • Gas: Has neither definite shape nor volume and expands to fill its container (like air or water vapor)
Ice (solid) melting into water (liquid) - an example of a phase change where matter is conserved

How Matter Changes Form

Matter can change from one state to another through various processes, but the total amount of matter always remains the same. Let's explore some common examples:

Experiment 1: Dissolving Sugar in Water

When you add 25 grams of sugar to 150 grams of water and stir, the sugar seems to disappear! But has it really vanished?

What happens: The sugar dissolves in the water, breaking down into tiny particles that spread throughout the water. Even though you can't see the sugar anymore, it's still there!

The evidence: If you weigh the sugar water, it will weigh 175 grams (25g sugar + 150g water). This proves the sugar didn't disappear—it just changed form.

Further proof: If you boil the water away, the sugar will reappear as crystals. The sugar was there the whole time!

Sugar crystals reappear after the water has evaporated, showing that the sugar was present all along

When a substance dissolves, it doesn't disappear—it simply changes form and becomes evenly distributed throughout the liquid. The total mass remains the same!

Experiment 2: Boiling Water

When water boils, it changes from a liquid to a gas called water vapor.

What happens: As the water heats up, its molecules gain energy and move faster until they escape into the air as water vapor. It may seem like the water is disappearing, but it's just changing form.

The evidence: If you boil water in an open pot, the water level decreases. But if you could capture all the water vapor and weigh it, it would weigh exactly the same as the original liquid water.

Water boiling and changing from liquid to gas (water vapor). The water appears to disappear but is actually changing state.

Experiment 3: Dry Ice Transformation

Dry ice is frozen carbon dioxide. Unlike regular ice, dry ice doesn't melt into a liquid—it transforms directly from a solid to a gas in a process called sublimation.

What happens: The dry ice appears to be "smoking" as it transforms, but that's actually the cold carbon dioxide gas becoming visible when it condenses water vapor in the air.

The evidence: If you place dry ice in a sealed container with a balloon on top, the balloon will inflate as the dry ice turns to gas. If you weigh the entire setup before and after, the weight remains the same—proving that no matter was lost or gained!

Phase changes (solid → liquid → gas) don't create or destroy matter. The total amount of matter stays the same, even when it changes form!

Chemical Reactions and Conservation of Matter

Chemical reactions create new substances, but they still follow the Law of Conservation of Matter. Let's see how:

Experiment 4: Creating a "Fire Snake"

When you mix sugar and baking soda and heat them, a chemical reaction occurs that produces a snake-like foam that's much larger than the original mixture.

What happens: The heat causes the sugar to caramelize and the baking soda to release carbon dioxide gas. These gases form bubbles that expand and create the snake-like structure.

The evidence: If you weigh the ingredients before (let's say 25 grams total), and then weigh the "snake" afterward (about 23 grams), you'll notice a small difference. This is because some carbon dioxide gas escaped into the air. If you could capture and weigh all the gases released, the total would still be 25 grams!

Experiment 5: Burning a Candle

When a candle burns, it appears to slowly disappear. Is matter being destroyed?

What happens: The heat of the flame melts the wax, which is then drawn up the wick. The hot wax reacts with oxygen in the air, producing carbon dioxide gas, water vapor, and heat energy.

The evidence: If you burn a candle in a sealed container and could capture all the gases produced, the total weight would be the same as the original candle plus the oxygen used in burning.

A burning candle - the wax appears to disappear but is actually changing into carbon dioxide gas and water vapor

Experiment 6: Burning Wood and Coal

When wood or coal burns, it seems to be destroyed and leaves behind only a small amount of ash. Where does all the matter go?

What happens: During combustion, the carbon in the wood or coal combines with oxygen from the air to form carbon dioxide gas, while hydrogen combines with oxygen to form water vapor. Other elements form various gases and solid ash.

The evidence: If you could capture all gases released during burning and weigh them along with the ash, the total weight would equal the original wood or coal plus the oxygen used in the reaction.

A burning log converts most of its mass into gases that escape into the air
Burning coal changes form but conserves its matter through the reaction with oxygen

When something burns, matter is not destroyed! It changes form, often into gases that we cannot see. The total mass of all products equals the total mass of all reactants.

Experiment 7: Baking a Cake

When you bake a cake, it rises and gets bigger. Is new matter being created?

What happens: Baking powder or yeast in the batter produces carbon dioxide gas. These gas bubbles expand when heated, making the cake rise and appear larger.

The evidence: Although the cake looks larger, it hasn't gained mass. If you weigh all ingredients before baking and weigh the cake afterward, the cake will actually weigh slightly less (due to some water evaporating). No new matter was created—the cake just expanded due to gas bubbles!

A cake rising in the oven - the increased volume comes from expanding gas bubbles, not creation of new matter

Testing Conservation of Matter

Scientists test the Law of Conservation of Matter by carefully weighing materials before and after a change occurs. Here's how:

  1. Weigh all materials involved before the change
  2. Make sure to use a closed system if gases might be produced (to capture everything)
  3. Weigh everything after the change
  4. Compare the before and after weights—they should be the same!

If weights before and after don't match exactly in an experiment, it usually means some matter escaped (often as a gas) and wasn't measured—not that matter was created or destroyed!

Key Vocabulary

Matter: Anything that has weight and takes up space

Law of Conservation of Matter: States that the amount of matter stays the same even when a substance changes form

Dissolve: To mix one substance into another substance, creating a solution (like salt in water)

Gram: A scientific unit for measuring weight or mass (a U.S. dollar bill weighs about 1 gram)

Dry Ice: Solid carbon dioxide that changes directly from a solid to a gas without becoming a liquid first

Conserve: To keep the same, to save, to not lose

Phase Change: When matter changes from one state to another (solid, liquid, gas)

Chemical Reaction: A process that creates new substances with different properties

Combustion: A chemical reaction where a substance combines with oxygen, often producing flame, heat, and light

Sublimation: The process of changing directly from a solid to a gas without becoming a liquid first

Test Your Knowledge

Quiz Question 1

If you dissolve 10 grams of salt in 100 grams of water, what will the total weight of the solution be?

110 grams. According to the Law of Conservation of Matter, the weight of the salt plus the weight of the water equals the weight of the solution (10g + 100g = 110g). The salt doesn't disappear—it just dissolves!

Quiz Question 2

When dry ice (solid carbon dioxide) sublimates (changes from a solid directly to a gas), what happens to the total amount of matter?

The total amount of matter stays the same. The carbon dioxide changes form from a solid to a gas, but no matter is created or destroyed. If you could capture all the gas in a balloon and weigh it, it would weigh the same as the original dry ice.

Quiz Question 3

When a 50-gram candle burns completely, the remaining wax weighs only 5 grams. Does this violate the Law of Conservation of Matter?

No, it doesn't violate the law. The "missing" 45 grams didn't disappear—they were converted to carbon dioxide gas and water vapor that escaped into the air. If you could capture all these gases and weigh them (plus the remaining wax), the total would still be 50 grams.

Quiz Question 4

When you bake bread, it rises and becomes much larger than the original dough. Does this mean new matter was created?

No, new matter wasn't created. The bread appears larger because yeast produces carbon dioxide gas, which forms bubbles that expand when heated. This makes the bread rise and appear larger, but the total amount of matter remains essentially the same (some water may evaporate during baking).

Quiz Question 5

A student mixes baking soda (5g) and vinegar (20g) in an open container. After the fizzing stops, the mixture weighs 22g. What happened to the "missing" 3 grams?

The baking soda and vinegar reacted to produce carbon dioxide gas, which escaped into the air. The "missing" 3 grams represents this escaped gas. If the reaction had taken place in a sealed container that captured the gas, the total weight would still be 25 grams (5g + 20g).

Quiz Question 6

When 100 grams of ice melts completely to form liquid water, what will be the weight of the resulting water?

100 grams. When ice melts, it changes from a solid state to a liquid state, but the amount of matter (water molecules) remains the same. The Law of Conservation of Matter ensures that the mass is conserved during this phase change.

Quiz Question 7

A 200-gram log burns almost completely, leaving only 5 grams of ash. What happened to the other 195 grams of matter?

The 195 grams were converted into gases (mainly carbon dioxide and water vapor) that escaped into the air. The wood didn't disappear—it changed form through a chemical reaction with oxygen from the air. If you could capture all these gases and weigh them along with the ash, the total would equal 200 grams plus the weight of the oxygen that combined with the wood.

Summary: Key Points to Remember

  1. Matter is anything that has weight and takes up space.
  2. The Law of Conservation of Matter states that matter cannot be created or destroyed; it can only change form.
  3. When substances dissolve, they're still there even if you can't see them.
  4. When matter changes state (solid → liquid → gas), no matter is lost or gained.
  5. During chemical reactions, new substances form, but the total amount of matter stays the same.
  6. In an open system, matter may seem to disappear because gases can escape, but in a closed system, the total weight remains constant.
  7. Weighing substances before and after a change is a good way to demonstrate conservation of matter.
  8. When something appears larger (like bread rising), it doesn't mean new matter was created—it's just expanded due to gas bubbles.
  9. When substances burn, they combine with oxygen to form gases and sometimes ash, but the total matter is conserved.
  10. Everyday examples like melting ice, boiling water, dissolving sugar, burning candles, and baking cakes all demonstrate conservation of matter.
