Food Webs: Cycling of Matter and Flow of Energy

Welcome to our exploration of ecosystems, food webs, and the amazing cycling of matter and flow of energy that occurs in our natural world! In this lesson, we'll discover how energy moves through living things and how matter is recycled in nature.

Understanding Ecosystems

An ecosystem is a system made up of living (biotic) and nonliving (abiotic) components that interact with each other in a particular environment. The living things within an ecosystem depend on each other to obtain energy needed for survival.

Every ecosystem on Earth, whether it's a forest, ocean, desert, or even a small pond, contains organisms that interact with each other and their environment. These interactions often involve the transfer of energy and cycling of matter.

Food Chains: A Simple View of Energy Transfer

Let's start with a simple concept called a food chain. A food chain is a straightforward diagram that shows how energy flows from one organism to another in an ecosystem.

A simple food chain showing the flow of energy from producers (plants) to primary consumers (herbivores) to secondary and tertiary consumers (carnivores).

A typical food chain has four levels:

  1. Producers (plants) - at the bottom of the chain
  2. First-order consumers (herbivores) - eat the plants
  3. Second-order consumers (carnivores) - eat the herbivores
  4. Tertiary consumers (top predators) - eat the second-order consumers

For example, in a grassland ecosystem, a simple food chain might be: grass → grasshopper → frog → hawk. The grass is the producer, the grasshopper is the first-order consumer, the frog is the second-order consumer, and the hawk is the tertiary consumer.

Food Webs: The Complex Reality

In nature, things are rarely as simple as a straight-line food chain. Most organisms eat more than one type of food, and many are eaten by multiple predators. This creates a complex network called a food web.

A food web is the overlapping of multiple food chains within an ecosystem. It shows how matter cycles and energy flows through the various feeding relationships.

For example, bears are omnivores that eat plants (like berries) as well as animals (like fish). This means they participate in multiple food chains, which creates connections in the food web. Similarly, a mouse might be eaten by a snake, an owl, or a fox—creating multiple pathways in the food web.

Producers: The Foundation of All Food Webs

At the base of every food web are organisms called producers. Producers are critical to ecosystems because they are the only organisms that can make their own food using energy from the sun.

Producers are organisms (usually green plants) that can produce their own food through a process called photosynthesis. They capture light energy from the sun and convert it into chemical energy stored in sugar molecules.

During photosynthesis, plants use:

  • Energy from sunlight
  • Carbon dioxide from the air
  • Water from the soil

And they produce:

  • Sugar (glucose) - which they use for energy
  • Oxygen - which is released into the atmosphere

The chemical equation for photosynthesis is:

6 CO2 + 6 H2O + sunlight → C6H12O6 + 6 O2

(carbon dioxide + water + sunlight → glucose + oxygen)

Without producers, there would be no food for consumers, and ecosystems would collapse. This is why protecting plant life on Earth is so crucial.

Consumers: Getting Energy from Others

Unlike producers, consumers cannot make their own food. They must eat other organisms to obtain energy.

Consumers are organisms that get their energy by consuming (eating) other organisms. They cannot make their own food.

Consumers are classified based on what they eat:

  • Herbivores: Eat only plants (primary consumers)
  • Carnivores: Eat only animals (secondary or tertiary consumers)
  • Omnivores: Eat both plants and animals
  • Apex consumers: Predators at the top of a food chain with no natural predators

Cellular Respiration: How Organisms Use Food Energy

When animals consume food, their bodies undergo a process called cellular respiration to release the energy stored in food molecules.

Food webs rely on the complementary processes of photosynthesis (in plants) and cellular respiration (in all organisms). These processes form the foundation of energy flow in ecosystems.

Cellular respiration is the process that both plants and animals use to convert sugar and oxygen into carbon dioxide, water, and energy.

The equation for cellular respiration is:

C6H12O6 + 6 O2 → 6 CO2 + 6 H2O + energy

(glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + energy)

Notice how cellular respiration is essentially the opposite of photosynthesis. This illustrates how matter cycles between processes in an ecosystem.

Energy Flow Through Ecosystems

Energy flows in one direction through ecosystems, starting with the sun. The sun provides energy for producers through photosynthesis, and then that energy is passed along to consumers when they eat the producers.

An ecological energy pyramid showing how energy decreases at each trophic level. Only about 10% of energy transfers from one level to the next, which explains why there are fewer organisms at higher trophic levels.

Energy transfer is inefficient. Only about 10% of the energy available at one trophic level is transferred to the next level. The rest is lost as heat through cellular respiration or is not consumed.

This inefficiency in energy transfer creates what's known as an energy pyramid. Because only 10% of energy passes to the next level, there must be more organisms at lower levels to support fewer organisms at higher levels.

For example:

  • 100% energy at producer level (thousands of plants)
  • 10% energy at primary consumer level (hundreds of herbivores)
  • 1% energy at secondary consumer level (dozens of small carnivores)
  • 0.1% energy at tertiary consumer level (a few apex predators)

This is why we see many plants, fewer herbivores, even fewer small carnivores, and very few apex predators in an ecosystem.

Decomposers: Nature's Recyclers

When organisms die, their bodies still contain valuable matter. Decomposers break down this dead material and return nutrients to the ecosystem.

Decomposers like fungi, bacteria, and certain insects break down dead organic matter, returning important nutrients to the soil.

Decomposers are organisms such as fungi, bacteria, worms, and some insects that break down dead plants and animals into simpler substances, returning nutrients to the soil, water, and air.

Examples of decomposers include:

  • Fungi (like mushrooms)
  • Bacteria
  • Earthworms
  • Millipedes
  • Dung beetles

Decomposers in action: Breaking down dead leaves and organic matter into nutrients that can be reused by plants and other organisms.

Without decomposers, dead organisms would pile up, and the nutrients they contain would remain locked away, unavailable for use by new living organisms.

The Cycling of Matter in Ecosystems

Unlike energy, which flows through an ecosystem in one direction, matter cycles continuously through ecosystems. The same atoms that make up your body today may have once been part of a dinosaur, a tree, or even a star!

Matter is neither created nor destroyed in an ecosystem—it is conserved and recycled. The same atoms are used over and over again, moving from the environment to living things and back to the environment.

Here's how it works:

  1. Plants take in carbon dioxide from the air and water and nutrients from the soil.
  2. Plants use these materials to build their cells through photosynthesis.
  3. Animals eat plants and use the matter to build their own cells.
  4. When plants and animals die, decomposers break down their remains.
  5. This decomposition releases carbon dioxide back into the air and returns nutrients to the soil.
  6. These materials can then be used by new plants, and the cycle continues.

This cycling occurs for many elements, including carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and water.

Try to follow a single carbon atom through an ecosystem. It might start in the air as CO2, then become part of a plant sugar molecule through photosynthesis, then part of a rabbit that eats the plant, then part of a fox that eats the rabbit, and finally released back into the air as CO2 when the fox breathes or when its body decomposes after death.

Bioaccumulation: When Harmful Substances Build Up

Not all substances that cycle through ecosystems are beneficial. Some harmful substances, like certain pesticides or heavy metals such as mercury, can build up in organisms through a process called bioaccumulation.

Bioaccumulation occurs when substances (often pollutants) are taken up by organisms faster than they can be broken down or excreted. These substances become more concentrated as they move up the food chain.

Marine biologists have discovered that mercury levels can be very high in large predatory fish like tuna and swordfish. This happens because:

  1. Tiny organisms absorb small amounts of mercury from the water
  2. Small fish eat many of these tiny organisms, accumulating more mercury
  3. Medium-sized fish eat many small fish, accumulating even more mercury
  4. Large predatory fish eat many medium-sized fish, accumulating very high levels of mercury

Marine biologists study ocean food webs and can help us understand issues like mercury bioaccumulation in marine ecosystems.

This is why pregnant women are often advised to limit consumption of certain fish - the mercury levels could be harmful to developing babies.

Impacts of Changes to Food Webs

Food webs can be disrupted by various factors, including:

  • Introduction of non-native species
  • Removal of key species (especially predators)
  • Habitat destruction
  • Climate change
  • Pollution

When one part of a food web is affected, it can have ripple effects throughout the entire ecosystem. For example:

When wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park, they reduced the elk population. With fewer elk eating young tree saplings, more trees grew to maturity. This provided habitat for birds, food for beavers (who built dams that created wetlands), and even changed the flow of rivers.

This example shows how removing or adding even one species can dramatically change an ecosystem.

Real-World Applications: Careers Studying Food Webs

Understanding food webs is important for many careers:

  • Marine Biologists study ocean food webs and how pollutants like mercury move through marine ecosystems
  • Ecologists examine how changes in one part of a food web affect the entire ecosystem
  • Conservation Biologists work to protect endangered species and their roles in food webs
  • Agricultural Scientists study how to grow food efficiently while minimizing ecosystem disruption
  • Registered Dieticians help people understand nutritional needs and design healthy meal plans

Vocabulary Review

Ecosystem: A system of living and nonliving things in a particular area or environment.

Producer: A living thing that produces its own food (usually plants using photosynthesis).

Consumer: A living thing that eats other living things to obtain energy.

Decomposer: A group of living things that break down dead organisms into simpler substances.

Food Chain: A simple diagram showing how energy moves from one living thing to another.

Food Web: A model showing how matter cycles and energy flows in an ecosystem through the intertwining of food chains.

Photosynthesis: The process by which plants use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to create oxygen and energy in the form of sugar.

Cellular Respiration: The process by which organisms break down glucose using oxygen to release energy, carbon dioxide, and water.

Trophic Level: Each step in a food chain or food web.

Apex Consumer: A predator at the top of a food chain with no natural predators of its own.

Bioaccumulation: The buildup of substances (often pollutants) in organisms as they move up the food chain.

Key Takeaways

  1. Food webs show the complex feeding relationships in ecosystems.
  2. Energy flows in one direction through ecosystems, starting with the sun and moving through producers to consumers.
  3. Only about 10% of energy transfers from one trophic level to the next, creating an energy pyramid with fewer organisms at higher levels.
  4. Matter cycles continuously through ecosystems, moving from the environment to living things and back again.
  5. Producers create their own food through photosynthesis, while consumers must eat other organisms.
  6. Decomposers break down dead organisms, returning nutrients to the environment.
  7. Changes to one part of a food web can affect the entire ecosystem.
  8. Harmful substances can bioaccumulate in food chains, becoming more concentrated at higher trophic levels.
