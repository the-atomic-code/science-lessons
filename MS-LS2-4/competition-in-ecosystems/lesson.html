Competition in Ecosystems

Introduction to Competition in Ecosystems

In the complex world of nature, organisms must constantly struggle to obtain what they need to survive. This struggle is known as competition, and it plays a crucial role in maintaining the balance and health of ecosystems.

What is Competition?

Competition is the relationship between organisms that strive for the same resources in the same ecosystem. These resources are limited, meaning there isn't enough for everyone, which forces organisms to compete.

Resources and Competition

All living things need certain resources to survive, grow, and reproduce. These resources can include:

  • Food - energy source for animals
  • Water - essential for all life processes
  • Shelter - protection from predators and harsh conditions
  • Space - area to live, hunt, or grow
  • Sunlight - energy source for plants
  • Air - oxygen for animals, carbon dioxide for plants
  • Soil nutrients - essential elements for plant growth

Plants competing for sunlight, water, and space in their environment

Example: Plant Competition

Plants compete with each other for water, sunlight, space, and soil nutrients. As shown in the image above, when plants grow near each other, they compete for available sunlight and space. Some plants will grow taller or spread their leaves wider to capture more sunlight, potentially shading out smaller plants. Plants with more extensive root systems can access more water and nutrients, giving them a competitive advantage.

Example: Animal Competition

When resources like food become limited, faster or stronger animals often have an advantage. For instance, if there are limited small prey animals in an area, foxes that can run faster will catch more food than slower foxes. Over time, the slower foxes might not get enough food to survive or reproduce.

Environmental Factors and Competition

Environmental factors can dramatically affect how organisms compete for resources. These factors include both living (biotic) and non-living (abiotic) components of an ecosystem.

Environmental Factors Include:

  • Temperature
  • Rainfall and water availability
  • Soil quality
  • Weather events (floods, droughts, fires)
  • Natural disasters
  • Human activities (pollution, habitat destruction)
  • Presence of other species

Example: Drought Effects

During a drought, water becomes even more limited than usual. Plants that have deeper root systems can reach water deeper in the soil, giving them an advantage over plants with shallow roots. Animals may need to travel farther to find water sources, which requires more energy and exposes them to more dangers.

Example: Flood Effects

When flooding occurs in an ecosystem, it affects the available resources. Plants may be washed away, reducing food sources for herbivores. Animals may lose their shelters, making them more vulnerable to predators. The entire balance of the ecosystem changes, forcing organisms to compete differently for the remaining resources.

Genetic Factors in Competition

Not all organisms are equally equipped to compete for resources. Genetic factors—traits inherited from parents—play a crucial role in determining how well an organism can compete.

Important Genetic Factors:

  • Physical traits - size, strength, speed, leg length
  • Camouflage - fur or feather coloration that helps hide from predators
  • Specialized features - beaks, claws, teeth adapted for specific foods
  • Resistance - tolerance to extreme temperatures, diseases, or toxins
  • Behavioral traits - hunting strategies, mating displays, territorial behaviors

A bee-eater bird displaying specialized hunting adaptations to catch insects

Example: Bird Adaptations

As shown in the image above, birds like the bee-eater have specialized genetic adaptations that give them advantages in hunting. Their sharp vision helps them spot insects in flight, and their specialized beaks allow them to catch and handle prey like bees without getting stung. The stunning coloration seen in the bee-eater serves multiple purposes - it can attract mates or help with species recognition, but may also make them more visible to predators, showing the tradeoffs that often come with genetic adaptations.

Food Webs and Energy Transfer

To understand competition fully, we need to look at how energy moves through an ecosystem using a model called a food web.

Food Web Basics:

A food web is a model that shows the complex feeding relationships between organisms in an ecosystem. It demonstrates how energy and matter are transferred from one organism to another.

In a food web:

  • Arrows point from the organism being eaten to the organism doing the eating
  • Energy flows from producers (usually plants) to consumers (animals)
  • Primary consumers eat producers
  • Secondary consumers eat primary consumers
  • Tertiary consumers eat secondary consumers
  • Decomposers break down dead organisms and return nutrients to the soil

A food web showing the flow of energy through different trophic levels in an ecosystem

Example: Food Web Relationships

In the food web shown above, you can see how energy flows from producers at the bottom to various levels of consumers. The arrows show the direction of energy transfer. When one population changes in this web, it affects multiple other populations. For example, if the population of a predator increases, it might reduce the populations of several prey species, which in turn affects what those prey species eat.

Invasive Species and Ecosystem Disruption

One of the most significant ways ecosystems can be disrupted is through the introduction of invasive species.

What Are Invasive Species?

Invasive species are organisms that are not native to an ecosystem and whose introduction causes or is likely to cause economic or environmental harm or harm to human health. These species often lack natural predators in their new environment, allowing their populations to grow unchecked.

Feral pigs, an invasive species in many ecosystems, with their offspring

Example: Feral Pigs

As shown in the image above, feral pigs can reproduce quickly and establish growing populations. These pigs were introduced to many regions by early explorers as a food source. Without natural predators, their populations have grown rapidly in many areas. These pigs dig up soil looking for food, which can destroy native plant communities. They compete with native animals for food resources and can even prey on small animals. Their activities can lead to soil erosion and water pollution. A single group of feral pigs can damage large areas of habitat in their search for food.

Example: Invasive Plants

Invasive plants can outcompete native species for sunlight, water, and nutrients. For example, when an aggressive non-native vine grows in a forest, it might quickly spread across the ground and up trees, blocking sunlight from reaching native plants. As the native plants die off, the animals that depended on those plants for food or shelter are also affected.

Population Dynamics and Ecosystem Balance

The size of a population in an ecosystem is affected by many factors and can change dramatically over time.

Factors Affecting Population Size:

  • Resource availability - food, water, shelter, space
  • Predator-prey relationships - changes in one affect the other
  • Disease - can rapidly reduce population numbers
  • Competition - both within species and between different species
  • Environmental changes - both short-term and long-term
  • Human activities - habitat destruction, pollution, hunting
  • Natural disasters - fires, floods, droughts

Example: Predator-Prey Dynamics

When a predator population increases, it puts more pressure on prey populations, which may decrease. As prey becomes scarcer, predators may have less food, causing their populations to decline. With fewer predators, prey populations can increase again. This creates a cyclical pattern often seen in nature.

The Role of Wildlife Biologists in Studying Ecosystems

Scientists play a crucial role in understanding and managing ecosystems. Wildlife biologists are specially trained to study animal populations and their interactions with their environments.

Career infographic showing the work and importance of wildlife biologists

What Wildlife Biologists Do:

As illustrated in the infographic above, wildlife biologists perform many important tasks:

  • Track population sizes of various species
  • Study animal behavior and habitat requirements
  • Monitor endangered species
  • Manage wildlife in protected areas
  • Develop conservation strategies
  • Study the impacts of human activities on wildlife
  • Help relocate animals when populations become too large for their habitat
  • Conduct field research in various ecosystems
  • Use technology to track animal movements and behaviors
  • Analyze data to understand population trends

Example: Managing Overpopulation

When deer populations become too large for an area to support, wildlife biologists might recommend controlled hunting or relocation programs. Without such management, the deer might overgraze the area, causing damage to plant communities, which in turn affects other animals in the ecosystem.

A marine biologist studying marine life in an ocean ecosystem

Example: Marine Biologists

As shown in the image above, marine biologists often conduct research underwater to study marine ecosystems. This specialized field of biology focuses on ocean ecosystems and the organisms that live there. Marine biologists might study how pollution affects coral reefs, track migration patterns of large ocean animals, or monitor the health of fish populations that are important for commercial fishing. Their work is crucial for understanding and protecting the ocean's biodiversity.

The Impact of Small Changes on Ecosystems

One fascinating aspect of ecosystems is how small changes can sometimes lead to large effects throughout the system.

Cascade Effects:

When one part of an ecosystem changes, it can create a domino effect that impacts many other parts. This is often called a cascade effect or a trophic cascade when it occurs through food web relationships.

Example: Temperature Changes in Aquatic Ecosystems

Even a small increase in water temperature can reduce oxygen levels in a stream or lake. This can stress fish populations, making them more susceptible to disease. If fish populations decline, the organisms that prey on them have less food. Additionally, if the fish were controlling insect populations, those insects might increase in number, affecting other parts of the ecosystem.

Example: Pollinator Decline

A small decrease in bee populations can lead to reduced pollination of flowering plants. This can result in fewer seeds and fruits being produced. Animals that rely on these fruits and seeds for food may then face food shortages, affecting their populations. This chain reaction continues throughout the ecosystem.

Habitat Fragmentation and Genetic Diversity

The physical structure of habitats plays a crucial role in supporting biodiversity and enabling species to adapt to changing conditions.

Habitat Fragmentation:

When large areas of habitat are broken into smaller, isolated patches (often by human development), it's called habitat fragmentation. This can have significant impacts on the organisms living there.

Example: Genetic Isolation

When a population is split by habitat fragmentation, the smaller populations may become genetically isolated from each other. Over time, this reduces genetic diversity as animals breed with a smaller pool of individuals. Reduced genetic diversity makes populations less able to adapt to changes, such as new diseases or climate variations.

The Importance of Keystone Species

Some species have a disproportionately large effect on their environment relative to their abundance. These are called keystone species.

Keystone Species:

A keystone species is a plant or animal that plays a unique and crucial role in the way an ecosystem functions. Without keystone species, the ecosystem would be dramatically different or cease to exist altogether.

Example: Sea Otters

Sea otters prey on sea urchins, which eat kelp. When sea otter populations declined due to hunting, sea urchin populations exploded, leading to the destruction of kelp forests. This affected numerous other species that relied on kelp forests for food and shelter. When sea otters were protected and their populations recovered, kelp forests began to recover as well.

Climate Change and Ecosystems

Long-term changes in climate patterns can have profound effects on ecosystems and the competition for resources within them.

Climate Change Effects on Ecosystems:

  • Shifting ranges of plants and animals
  • Changes in timing of seasonal activities (migration, flowering)
  • Altered precipitation patterns affecting water availability
  • Rising temperatures affecting temperature-sensitive species
  • Increased frequency of extreme weather events
  • Rising sea levels affecting coastal ecosystems

Example: Coral Reef Bleaching

Coral reefs are highly sensitive to water temperature. Even small increases in ocean temperatures can cause corals to expel the algae living in their tissues, resulting in coral bleaching. This affects the entire reef ecosystem, as many fish and other marine creatures rely on healthy coral for food and shelter. Marine biologists, like the one shown in the earlier image, are at the forefront of studying these changes and developing conservation strategies.

Vocabulary Review

Competition
Relationship between organisms that strive for the same resources in the same ecosystem.
Resources
A substance or object in the environment required by an organism for normal growth, maintenance, and reproduction.
Organism
Any living thing.
Environment
Surroundings or conditions in which a person, animal, or plant lives.
Environmental Factors
The things in one's surroundings, including living and nonliving things.
Genetic Factors
Traits that are inherited from one's parents, such as hair color, eye color, and height.
Food Web
A model showing the complex feeding relationships between organisms in an ecosystem.
Invasive Species
Organisms that are not native to an ecosystem whose introduction causes harm.
Ecosystem
A community of living organisms in conjunction with the nonliving components of their environment.
Population
All the organisms of the same group or species who live in a particular geographical area.
Habitat
The natural home or environment of an animal, plant, or other organism.
Keystone Species
A species on which other species in an ecosystem largely depend, such that if it were removed the ecosystem would change drastically.
