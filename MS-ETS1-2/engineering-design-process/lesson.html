In this lesson, we'll explore how engineers solve problems using a systematic approach called the engineering design process. You'll learn about the three main phases of this process, understand important engineering terms like criteria and constraints, and discover how engineers test and improve their designs.

Introduction to Engineering Design

Have you ever wondered how engineers create solutions to problems? Whether it's designing faster race cars, developing machines to help doctors perform surgery, or finding ways to reduce pollution, engineers follow a creative and systematic approach to solve problems.

The engineering design process is a series of steps that provides a framework for finding solutions to problems. Though the exact number of steps may vary from 6 to 19 depending on who you ask, all engineering design processes share three main phases:

The three main phases of the engineering design process: Define the Problem, Develop Ideas, and Optimize the Design Solution.

  1. Define the problem
  2. Develop ideas
  3. Optimize the design solution

Unlike following a recipe where steps are completed in exact order, the engineering design process is iterative. This means engineers might repeat steps or move back and forth between them as needed. The goal is finding the best solution, not simply following steps in order.

Phase 1: Define the Problem

The first phase of the engineering design process involves clearly understanding the problem that needs to be solved. Engineers may be assigned a specific problem or might observe the world around them to identify issues needing solutions.

Engineers collaborating to define a problem, identify requirements, and establish constraints before beginning the design process.

During this phase, engineers must identify two crucial elements:

Criteria: The requirements that must be met for a design solution to be considered successful. Think of these as the "must-haves" or goals of the project.

Constraints: The limitations placed on the design solution. These might include budget restrictions, available materials, time limitations, or physical requirements.

Together, criteria and constraints help engineers evaluate whether their final solution is successful. They also provide guidelines for comparing different potential solutions.

Example: Bridge Design

Problem: Design a bridge to cross a river

Criteria: Must support at least 10,000 pounds, must allow boats to pass underneath, must withstand flooding

Constraints: Budget of $2 million, must be completed within 1 year, must use locally available materials

Phase 2: Develop Ideas

Once engineers understand the problem, they begin developing possible solutions. This phase involves two main activities: brainstorming and research.

Engineers engaged in brainstorming sessions, researching information, and developing initial design concepts.

Brainstorming

Brainstorming is an informal, creative process used to generate ideas. During brainstorming:

  • Engineers sketch and label diagrams
  • All ideas are documented without judgment
  • The goal is quantity, not quality of ideas
  • Even unusual or seemingly impossible ideas are recorded

Brainstorming can be done individually or collaboratively in groups. The key is to get all ideas on paper!

Research

After brainstorming, engineers conduct research to enhance their ideas and determine if similar solutions already exist. Research may include:

  • Using online and print resources
  • Testing different materials
  • Interviewing experts in relevant fields
  • Talking to potential users of the product

Engineers often move back and forth between brainstorming and research as new information inspires additional ideas.

Developing Solutions

Once engineers have collected ideas through brainstorming and research, they begin to develop their most promising concepts into potential solutions. This might involve:

An engineer developing a detailed 3D computer-aided design (CAD) model to visualize and test a potential solution.

  • Creating detailed diagrams with labels explaining inputs, outputs, and energy flow
  • Developing 3D computer-aided design (CAD) models that show how parts interact
  • Building physical models to test concepts

These activities help engineers select the best idea to move forward with for prototyping.

Phase 3: Optimize the Design Solution

In the final phase, engineers build, test, and improve their design until it successfully meets all the criteria and constraints.

Engineers testing and optimizing a prototype design, collecting data and making adjustments to improve performance.

Prototyping

A prototype is a preliminary model built to demonstrate and test the functionality of a design solution. Prototypes may be:

  • Full-scale or scaled-down versions
  • Made with different materials than the final product
  • Focused on testing specific aspects of the design

Testing and Data Collection

Engineers perform tests on their prototypes to collect data on how well they meet the established criteria and constraints. This might involve:

  • Measuring performance metrics
  • Testing durability under different conditions
  • Getting feedback from potential users
  • Comparing results against specific requirements

Design Improvement

Based on test results and data analysis, engineers make improvements to their design. This is where the iterative nature of the engineering design process becomes most evident. Engineers will:

  • Identify weaknesses in the current design
  • Develop solutions to address those weaknesses
  • Modify the prototype
  • Test again to see if the modifications improved performance

This cycle of testing and improvement continues until the design successfully meets all criteria and falls within all constraints.

Real-World Example: Optimizing a Wind Turbine

Engineers testing wind turbine blade designs might collect data on energy output at different wind speeds. After analyzing this data, they might modify the blade shape to capture more energy, then test again. If the new design produces more energy but is too heavy, they might change materials to reduce weight while maintaining strength. Each round of testing and modification brings them closer to an optimal design.

Evaluating Competing Solutions

Engineers often develop multiple potential solutions to a problem. Selecting the best option requires systematic evaluation based on how well each solution meets the criteria and constraints.

Using Decision Matrices

A decision matrix helps engineers compare different solutions by rating each one against the established criteria. Here's how it works:

Design Solution Criterion 1
(Weight: 3)		 Criterion 2
(Weight: 2)		 Criterion 3
(Weight: 1)		 Total Score
Solution A 4 (12) 3 (6) 5 (5) 23
Solution B 5 (15) 2 (4) 2 (2) 21
Solution C 3 (9) 5 (10) 4 (4) 23

In this example, Solutions A and C tied with the highest scores. Engineers would need to consider additional factors or adjust the weighting of criteria to make a final decision.

Trade-offs in Design

Every engineering solution involves trade-offs. For example:

  • A stronger material might be heavier
  • A faster solution might use more energy
  • A more effective solution might cost more

Engineers must carefully balance these trade-offs based on the specific priorities of their project.

Engineering Specializations

The field of engineering is incredibly diverse, with specialists focusing on different types of problems. Some examples include:

  • Mechanical Engineers: Work on machines, engines, and mechanical systems
  • Electrical Engineers: Design electrical systems, electronics, and power distribution
  • Civil Engineers: Design infrastructure like buildings, bridges, and water systems
  • Chemical Engineers: Develop processes for manufacturing chemicals and materials
  • Environmental Engineers: Create solutions for environmental challenges like pollution
  • Biomedical Engineers: Design medical devices and equipment

While these engineers specialize in different areas, they all use the engineering design process to solve problems in their fields.

Engineering Design Process Vocabulary

  • Engineering: The use of scientific, mathematic, and design principles to solve problems
  • Defining the problem: The first step of the engineering design process, which defines the purpose, criteria, and constraints of an engineering solution
  • Design criteria: The requirements for success of an engineering solution
  • Design constraints: The limitations placed on an engineering solution
  • Research: The gathering of information about ideas related to an engineering solution
  • Brainstorming: An informal creative process where all ideas are documented without judgment
  • Prototype: A preliminary model built to demonstrate and test functionality
  • Computer-aided design (CAD): 3D digital modeling software used to design and visualize products
  • Optimizing design solutions: The continual process of testing and improving a design solution
  • Iterative process: A process that involves repeating steps and not necessarily following a linear order
