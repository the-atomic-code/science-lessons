Food Webs: Cycling of Matter and Flow of Energy

Introduction to Ecosystems

Every ecosystem is made up of a variety of living and nonliving things. The living things within an ecosystem depend on each other to obtain their energy and matter. In this lesson, we'll explore how energy flows through ecosystems and how matter cycles within them.

Key Term: An ecosystem is a system of living and nonliving things in a particular area or environment that interact with each other.

Food Chains and Food Webs

A simple diagram called a food chain represents how energy flows in an ecosystem. A food chain starts with producers (plants) at the bottom. Next, a first order consumer (herbivore) eats the plants to obtain energy. Next in the chain is the second order consumer (carnivore), followed by a tertiary consumer (top predator).

A food chain showing the flow of energy from producers (plants) through primary, secondary, and tertiary consumers.

Food chains typically have 4 levels, though this can vary. Here's a simple example:

  1. Producer (grass)
  2. Primary Consumer (rabbit)
  3. Secondary Consumer (fox)
  4. Tertiary Consumer (hawk)

A food web is the overlapping of multiple food chains within an ecosystem. Living things don't eat the same thing all the time. Just like people, animals like variety in their diets. This results in living things eating from multiple food chains, which creates a food web.

For example, bears consume plants (acting as primary consumers), but also consume fish (acting as secondary or tertiary consumers). This flexibility creates complex food webs in ecosystems.

Producers: The Foundation of Food Chains

Producers are organisms that make their own food through a process called photosynthesis. Plants, algae, and some bacteria are producers.

Photosynthesis:

This chemical process uses energy from the sun to turn carbon dioxide and water into sugar and oxygen.

The chemical equation for photosynthesis is:

Carbon Dioxide + Water + Sunlight → Glucose (Sugar) + Oxygen

6CO2 + 6H2O + light energy → C6H12O6 + 6O2

During photosynthesis, plants capture the sun's energy and store it in the chemical bonds of glucose molecules. This conversion of light energy to chemical energy is fundamental to nearly all life on Earth.

Food webs rely on the complementary processes of photosynthesis (in plants) and cellular respiration (in all organisms).

Consumers: Getting Energy Second-Hand

Consumers are living things that eat other living things to obtain energy. They cannot make their own food.

Type of Consumer Description Examples
Primary Consumer (Herbivore) Eats only plants Rabbits, deer, grasshoppers
Secondary Consumer (Carnivore) Eats primary consumers Foxes, frogs, small birds
Tertiary Consumer (Top Predator) Eats secondary consumers Hawks, wolves, large fish
Omnivore Eats both plants and animals Bears, humans, raccoons

When consumers eat food, their bodies break down the food molecules through digestion and cellular respiration to extract energy and useful building blocks.

Cellular Respiration: How Organisms Release Energy from Food

Cellular Respiration:

This chemical process takes place in the cells of all living things. It breaks down glucose (sugar) using oxygen to release energy that cells can use.

The chemical equation for cellular respiration is:

Glucose (Sugar) + Oxygen → Carbon Dioxide + Water + Energy

C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + Energy

Notice that cellular respiration is essentially the reverse of photosynthesis. The products of photosynthesis (glucose and oxygen) become the reactants in cellular respiration, and the products of cellular respiration (carbon dioxide and water) are the reactants in photosynthesis.

Energy Flow Through Ecosystems

All ecosystems start with energy from the sun. Without sunlight, plants cannot undergo photosynthesis. Using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide, plants make sugar and release oxygen.

When a consumer eats a plant, it gains some energy from these sugars. When the next consumer in the food chain eats the first consumer, energy is passed from one consumer to the next.

The Energy Pyramid

The amount of energy decreases with each level of the food chain. Only about 10% of the energy from one level is transferred to the next level. The rest is lost as heat during the organism's life processes.

The ecological energy pyramid shows how energy decreases at each trophic level. Only about 10% of energy transfers to the next level, with the rest being lost as heat.

  • Producers (Base of pyramid): 100% of available energy
  • Primary Consumers: About 10% of producer energy
  • Secondary Consumers: About 1% of producer energy
  • Tertiary Consumers (Top of pyramid): About 0.1% of producer energy

Since the amount of energy decreases with each level of the food chain, ecosystems can only support smaller numbers of consumers as you go up the food chain. At the top of a food chain, there might only be one hawk. At the bottom of the food chain, there would be many plants.

Decomposers: Nature's Recyclers

Decomposers are organisms such as fungi, bacteria, worms, or maggots that break down dead organic matter. This is crucial to the cycling of matter in an ecosystem.

Decomposers like fungi, bacteria, and certain invertebrates break down dead organic matter and return nutrients to the soil.

As decomposers feed on dead animals and plants, they break down the complex molecules into simpler substances like carbon dioxide and water molecules that go back into the soil. The amount of matter in an ecosystem never changes; it just cycles through the ecosystem in different forms.

Decomposers breaking down dead organic matter, returning essential nutrients to the soil.

Without decomposers, dead organisms and waste would pile up, and the nutrients within them would remain locked up and unavailable for future generations of living things.

Conservation of Matter in Ecosystems

A fundamental principle in science is that matter is neither created nor destroyed in ordinary chemical reactions; it is only rearranged. This applies to ecosystems too!

Cycling of Matter:

The same atoms that make up your body today have been cycling through Earth's ecosystems for billions of years. They may have been part of a dinosaur, a tree, or countless other organisms throughout history.

When you eat food, the atoms in that food become part of your body. Your body breaks down the food molecules through digestion and reassembles the atoms into molecules that make up your cells and tissues.

For example, when you eat a hamburger:

  1. Digestion breaks down the proteins in the meat into amino acids
  2. Your body absorbs these amino acids
  3. Your cells use these amino acids to build new proteins for your muscles and other tissues

The atoms themselves don't change – they're just rearranged into different molecules.

Think About It: How are atoms conserved when a hawk digests a mouse? Are any atoms created or destroyed in this process?

Food Provides Both Matter and Energy

Food serves two crucial purposes for living organisms:

  1. Source of matter: The atoms and molecules in food become part of your body
  2. Source of energy: Breaking the chemical bonds in food molecules releases energy that powers life processes

Remember: Energy flows through an ecosystem (sun → producers → consumers) and is eventually lost as heat, while matter cycles within an ecosystem (producers → consumers → decomposers → back to soil).

Bioaccumulation in Food Chains

Sometimes harmful substances like pesticides, heavy metals (such as mercury), or other pollutants can enter food chains. When this happens, these substances can become more concentrated at higher trophic levels in a process called bioaccumulation.

Example: Mercury in Marine Food Chains

Mercury from industrial pollution can enter water bodies and be absorbed by tiny organisms. Small fish eat many of these tiny organisms, accumulating the mercury in their tissues. Larger fish eat many small fish, further concentrating the mercury. When humans or other top predators consume these larger fish, they may ingest dangerous levels of mercury.

Marine biologists study ocean food chains and can help identify potential hazards like mercury accumulation in fish that humans consume.

This is why marine biologists study food chains within ocean ecosystems. Their research helps us understand which fish might contain higher levels of harmful substances like mercury, allowing us to make safer choices about the fish we eat.

Think About It: Why do larger, predatory fish like tuna and swordfish often contain higher levels of mercury than smaller fish like sardines?

Careers Related to Food Chains

Several different careers involve studying food chains and the cycling of matter and flow of energy:

  • Ecologists study relationships between organisms and their environment, including food webs
  • Marine biologists study food chains within ocean ecosystems and can help identify potential hazards like mercury in fish
  • Environmental scientists analyze how pollutants move through food webs
  • Registered dieticians help people make healthy food choices and create meal plans that meet specific dietary needs
  • Agricultural scientists work to improve food production while minimizing environmental impacts

Summary: The Big Ideas

  1. Energy flows through ecosystems in one direction, starting with the sun
  2. Matter cycles within ecosystems and is never created or destroyed
  3. Producers make their own food through photosynthesis
  4. Consumers get energy by eating other organisms
  5. Decomposers break down dead matter, returning nutrients to the soil
  6. Only about 10% of energy transfers to the next trophic level
  7. Food provides both matter (building blocks) and energy for organisms
  8. Photosynthesis and cellular respiration are complementary processes
