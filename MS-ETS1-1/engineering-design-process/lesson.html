The Engineering Design Process

Welcome to our lesson on the Engineering Design Process! In this lesson, you'll learn how engineers approach and solve problems in a structured, yet creative way. By the end, you'll understand the phases of the engineering design process and how engineers use criteria and constraints to develop successful solutions to real-world problems.

Engineers use the design process to solve a wide variety of problems

What is Engineering?

Before diving into the engineering design process, let's define what engineering actually is:

Engineering: The use of scientific, mathematic, and design principles to solve problems.

Engineers work in many different fields to solve a wide variety of problems. They might develop solutions to:

  • Reduce pollution
  • Create medical devices for surgeries
  • Design faster and more fuel-efficient vehicles
  • Develop renewable energy systems
  • Create recycling systems for plastic waste
  • Build bridges, buildings, and other structures

Engineering combines multiple disciplines - like this drone that requires mechanical, electrical, and software engineering

The Engineering Design Process: An Overview

The engineering design process is a series of steps that engineers follow to guide them in solving problems. While there are many versions of this process (ranging from 6 to 19 steps!), they all share three main phases:

The three main phases of the engineering design process

  1. Define the problem
  2. Develop ideas
  3. Optimize the design solution

An important characteristic of the engineering design process is that it's iterative. This means that engineers don't simply move through the steps in a straight line. Instead, they often repeat steps, jump back and forth between phases, and cycle through the process multiple times to improve their solutions.

Phase 1: Define the Problem

In the first phase, engineers clearly identify what problem needs to be solved. This might involve:

  • Being assigned a specific problem to work on
  • Observing the world around them to identify needs
  • Talking with potential users to understand their challenges

Engineers discussing and defining a problem before beginning to design solutions

Two critical components of problem definition are understanding the criteria and constraints:

Design criteria: The requirements needed to be met in order for the design idea to be considered a success.

Design constraints: The limitations that are placed on the design idea, such as cost, materials, time, or other restrictions.

Example: Water Filtration System

Imagine engineers designing a water filtration system for a rural community:

Criteria (requirements for success):

  • Must remove 99% of harmful bacteria
  • Must filter at least 100 gallons per day
  • Must be easy to use without special training

Constraints (limitations):

  • Must cost less than $200 to build
  • Must use locally available materials
  • Must operate without electricity
  • Must be completed within 6 months

Try It Yourself: Identifying Criteria and Constraints

Think about designing a solar-powered device to charge phones in areas without electricity. What might be some criteria and constraints?

Criteria might include: charges phones fully in less than 4 hours, works in partial sunlight, charges multiple devices simultaneously.

Constraints might include: must cost less than $50, must weigh less than 2 pounds, must be waterproof, must use available materials.

Phase 2: Develop Ideas

Once the problem is clearly defined, engineers begin developing possible solutions. This phase includes several activities:

Engineers brainstorming and researching to develop potential solutions

Brainstorming

Brainstorming: An informal, creative process used to generate ideas without judgment.

During brainstorming, engineers:

  • Work individually or in groups
  • Sketch and label diagrams
  • Take notes about potential solutions
  • Document all ideas without evaluating them (no idea is a "bad" idea during brainstorming!)

Research

Research: The gathering of information about ideas related to an engineering solution.

Research activities might include:

  • Using online or print resources
  • Testing materials
  • Interviewing experts or potential users
  • Investigating existing solutions to similar problems

Engineers often move back and forth between brainstorming and research, using new information to inspire more ideas.

Developing Solution Models

After brainstorming and research, engineers develop more detailed models of potential solutions:

  • Drawing detailed sketches with labels
  • Creating flowcharts showing inputs, outputs, and energy flow
  • Developing Computer-Aided Design (CAD) models

An engineer developing a detailed Computer-Aided Design (CAD) model

Computer-Aided Design (CAD): Software used to create precise 3D models showing how parts of a system move and interact.

Phase 3: Optimize the Design Solution

In the final phase, engineers build, test, and improve their design solutions.

Engineers testing and refining a prototype during the optimization phase

Prototyping

Prototype: A first, or preliminary, model built to demonstrate and test the functionality of a design solution.

Prototypes allow engineers to:

  • Test if the design works as intended
  • Identify problems or weaknesses
  • Collect data on performance
  • Show the design to potential users for feedback

Testing and Optimizing

Optimizing design solutions: The continual process of testing and improving a design solution.

During optimization, engineers:

  • Perform tests to see how well the design meets the criteria
  • Collect and analyze data
  • Identify ways to improve the design
  • Make changes and test again
  • Repeat until the design successfully meets all criteria within the constraints

Example: Electric Vehicle Design

Engineers designing an electric vehicle might optimize their design by:

  1. Building a prototype and testing its range on a single charge
  2. Analyzing data and discovering the battery loses efficiency in cold temperatures
  3. Redesigning the battery insulation system
  4. Testing the new design in different temperature conditions
  5. Further refining the design based on new test results
  6. Continuing this cycle until the vehicle meets all criteria (range, speed, safety) within constraints (cost, weight, manufacturing capabilities)

Think About It

Why is optimization so important in engineering design? Consider what might happen if engineers simply built their first design idea without going through multiple testing and improvement cycles.

Using Criteria and Constraints Throughout the Process

Criteria and constraints play a crucial role throughout the entire engineering design process:

  • During problem definition: They help clarify exactly what the solution needs to accomplish and within what limitations.
  • During idea development: They guide brainstorming and help engineers focus on feasible solutions.
  • During optimization: They provide the standards against which designs are tested and evaluated.
  • When comparing competing designs: They help engineers decide which solution is best.

Scenario Analysis: Bridge Design

A community needs a new bridge across a river. Consider these factors and determine which are criteria and which are constraints:

  • Must support vehicles up to 40 tons (Criterion)
  • Budget of $2 million (Constraint)
  • Must be completed within 18 months (Constraint)
  • Must withstand flooding (Criterion)
  • Cannot disturb protected wildlife habitat (Constraint)
  • Must include pedestrian walkway (Criterion)

Real-World Applications of the Engineering Design Process

The engineering design process is used in countless fields to solve a wide variety of problems:

Environmental Engineering

Environmental engineers might use the process to:

  • Design systems to reduce plastic waste in oceans
  • Create more efficient recycling processes
  • Develop flood prevention systems
  • Design water purification solutions

Biomedical Engineering

Biomedical engineers might use the process to:

  • Create new medical devices for surgery
  • Design prosthetic limbs
  • Develop diagnostic equipment
  • Create drug delivery systems

Civil Engineering

Civil engineers might use the process to:

  • Design earthquake-resistant buildings
  • Create energy-efficient structures
  • Develop transportation systems
  • Design water management infrastructure

Case Study: Waste Management Solution

Engineers designing a new waste management system for a city would need to consider multiple stakeholder perspectives:

  • Environmental: Minimize pollution and ecological impact
  • Economic: Keep costs affordable for the city and residents
  • Social: Ensure the system is convenient and acceptable to residents
  • Technical: Ensure the system can handle the volume and types of waste generated

Success criteria would need to balance all these perspectives, while constraints might include available land, budget limitations, and existing infrastructure.

Summary: Key Points About the Engineering Design Process

  • The engineering design process has three main phases: define the problem, develop ideas, and optimize the design solution.
  • The process is iterative - engineers repeat steps and move back and forth between phases as needed.
  • Design criteria are the requirements that must be met for success.
  • Design constraints are the limitations placed on the solution.
  • Brainstorming generates ideas without judgment.
  • Research gathers information to enhance understanding and inspire solutions.
  • Prototypes are preliminary models built to test functionality.
  • Optimization involves repeated testing and improving of designs.
  • Engineers consider multiple perspectives and stakeholder needs when defining problems and evaluating solutions.
