Chemical Reactions: Creating New Substances

A spectacular display of color changes during a chemical reaction, indicating the formation of new substances

Welcome to our exciting journey into the world of chemical reactions! In this lesson, we'll discover how substances transform into new materials with different properties, explore the difference between chemical and physical changes, and learn about the conservation of matter during reactions.

What is a Chemical Reaction?

A chemical reaction is a process in which new substances are formed. During this process, the atoms you start with rearrange themselves to form new substances with different properties. The key point to remember is that the number of atoms before and after a chemical reaction always stays the same.

Reactants (hydrogen and oxygen) rearrange their atoms to form products (water) in a chemical reaction

Think about it: When you burn wood in a campfire, the wood seems to disappear. But what's actually happening? The atoms in the wood are rearranging and combining with oxygen from the air to form new substances like carbon dioxide and water vapor. The wood hasn't vanished—its atoms have just rearranged!

Properties Change in Chemical Reactions

All matter has properties that can be observed and measured. These properties help us identify substances and predict how they might interact with other materials.

Properties of matter can be observed, recorded, and tested to identify substances

There are two main types of properties:

Physical Properties

  • Color
  • Density
  • Melting/boiling points
  • State (solid, liquid, gas)
  • Solubility
  • Malleability/Ductility

Chemical Properties

  • Flammability
  • Reactivity with water/acids
  • Oxidation (rusting)
  • Combustibility
  • Toxicity
  • Acidity/Basicity

When a chemical reaction occurs, the properties of the substances change because new substances with different properties are formed.

Detecting a Chemical Reaction

How can we tell if a chemical reaction has occurred? We can test the properties before and after the suspected reaction!

For example, if we test steel wool (which is mostly iron):

  • Before reaction: Steel wool is gray, attracted to magnets, and can burn when ignited.
  • After reaction with oxygen: The resulting iron oxide (rust) is reddish-brown, still somewhat attracted to magnets, but no longer flammable.

The changes in these properties tell us that a chemical reaction has taken place and a new substance has formed.

Conservation of Matter: Atoms Don't Disappear

The Law of Conservation of Matter states that matter cannot be created or destroyed in chemical reactions. This means that the total number of atoms before a reaction equals the total number after the reaction. Atoms simply rearrange themselves to form new substances.

The Law of Conservation of Matter: The total mass before a reaction equals the total mass after the reaction

If you measure the mass of all reactants before a chemical reaction and the mass of all products after the reaction (making sure to capture any gases formed), you'll find that the total mass remains exactly the same!

Here's how atoms rearrange in a chemical reaction:

4Fe + 3O2 → 2Fe2O3

In this reaction, iron (Fe) combines with oxygen (O2) to form iron oxide (Fe2O3). Notice that the number of iron atoms (4) and oxygen atoms (6) is the same on both sides of the equation. The atoms have just rearranged to form a new substance.

Atoms rearrange during chemical reactions while maintaining the same total number

Reactants and Products

In chemical reactions, we use specific terms to describe the substances involved:

  • Reactants: The starting substances that undergo the chemical reaction
  • Products: The new substances formed as a result of the reaction

Let's look at a common reaction - the combustion of propane in a gas grill:

C3H8 + 5O2 → 3CO2 + 4H2O

Reactants: Propane (C3H8) and oxygen (O2)

Products: Carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O)

Count the atoms on each side: 3 carbon atoms, 8 hydrogen atoms, and 10 oxygen atoms. The same number appears on both sides of the equation, but they're arranged differently to form new substances with different properties.

Chemical Changes vs. Physical Changes

Not all changes in matter involve chemical reactions. It's important to distinguish between chemical and physical changes:

The key difference: Chemical changes create new substances, physical changes don't

Chemical Changes Physical Changes
New substances form No new substances form
Chemical properties change Physical properties may change, but chemical properties remain the same
Often difficult or impossible to reverse Usually easily reversible
Examples: burning, rusting, cooking Examples: melting, freezing, boiling, breaking

Examples of chemical changes (like burning) and physical changes (like melting ice)

Important clarification: Many students think dissolving is a physical change, but it often involves chemical reactions! When a substance dissolves, it can break down or interact with the solvent, which can involve breaking and forming chemical bonds. This makes dissolving a chemical change in many cases.

Is It Physical or Chemical?

For each change, decide if it's physical or chemical:

  1. Ice melting into water (Physical - same H2O molecules, just arranged differently)
  2. A piece of paper burning (Chemical - new substances form, including carbon dioxide and water)
  3. Breaking a glass (Physical - same substance, just in smaller pieces)
  4. Milk souring (Chemical - bacteria convert lactose into lactic acid, creating new substances)
  5. Boiling water (Physical - water molecules remain H2O, just farther apart)

Molecules: Building Blocks of Substances

Molecules are formed when two or more atoms join together through chemical bonds. For example, oxygen gas (O2) consists of two oxygen atoms bonded together. Water (H2O) is a molecule made of two hydrogen atoms bonded to one oxygen atom.

During chemical reactions, molecules interact with each other, and their atoms rearrange to form new molecules with different properties. The specific arrangement of atoms in molecules determines the properties of substances.

Check Your Understanding:

What makes these two substances different?

- Oxygen (O2): a colorless, odorless gas necessary for breathing

- Ozone (O3): a bluish gas with a sharp odor that can be harmful to breathe

Both are made of only oxygen atoms, but they have different properties because of how the atoms are arranged in their molecules!

Catalysts: Speeding Up Reactions

A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being consumed in the reaction. Catalysts work by providing an alternative pathway for the reaction that requires less energy.

An important fact about catalysts: they help reactions happen faster, but they don't become part of the final products. After the reaction, you can recover the catalyst unchanged.

Examples of catalysts include:

  • Enzymes in your body that help break down food
  • Platinum in catalytic converters that help reduce harmful emissions
  • Manganese dioxide used to speed up the breakdown of hydrogen peroxide

Evidence of Chemical Reactions

How can we tell when a chemical reaction has occurred? Here are some common signs:

  1. Color change: A change in color often indicates new substances have formed.
  2. Production of gas: Bubbles or fizzing can indicate gas formation.
  3. Formation of a precipitate: A solid forming in a liquid solution.
  4. Temperature change: Heat being released (exothermic) or absorbed (endothermic).
  5. Light production: Light being emitted during the reaction.
  6. Change in properties: Changes in flammability, magnetism, electrical conductivity, etc.

Example: Baking Soda and Vinegar

When baking soda (sodium bicarbonate, NaHCO3) and vinegar (acetic acid, CH3COOH) are mixed, we observe:

  • Bubbling and fizzing (CO2 gas forms)
  • The mixture feels cold (the reaction is endothermic)
  • The pH changes (properties change)

These observations indicate that a chemical reaction has occurred, producing new substances with different properties.

Real-World Applications of Chemical Reactions

Chemical reactions are vital to our daily lives and many careers depend on understanding and using them:

  • Chemical Engineers: Design processes to efficiently create useful products
  • Material Scientists: Develop new materials with specific properties
  • Pharmacists: Work with chemical reactions to produce medicines
  • Food Scientists: Study chemical reactions in cooking and food preservation
  • Environmental Scientists: Analyze chemical reactions in ecosystems and pollution

Chemical Reactions All Around Us

Look for evidence of chemical reactions in your daily life:

  • Cooking: Browning of toast, rising of bread, caramelization of sugar
  • Batteries: Chemical reactions produce electricity
  • Fireworks: Chemical reactions produce colorful light and sounds
  • Digestion: Your body uses chemical reactions to break down food
  • Photosynthesis: Plants convert light energy, water, and carbon dioxide into glucose and oxygen

Important Terms to Remember

Term Definition
Atoms The smallest particle of matter made up of protons, neutrons, and electrons.
Molecules More than one atom combined to form a substance (e.g., oxygen – O2).
Reactants Substances that are combined to form products in a chemical reaction.
Products The result of substances being combined in a chemical reaction.
Chemical change Chemicals combining to cause new substances to be formed.
Physical change A change in state of matter where no new substance is formed.
Catalyst A substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being consumed in the reaction.

Summary

Chemical reactions are processes where atoms rearrange to form new substances with different properties. The Law of Conservation of Matter ensures that atoms are neither created nor destroyed during reactions—they simply rearrange. Chemical changes result in new substances forming, while physical changes only alter the appearance or state of a substance without creating something new.

By testing properties like flammability, magnetism, conductivity, and odor, we can determine if a chemical reaction has occurred. Understanding chemical reactions helps us make sense of the world around us and is crucial for many scientific and technological advances.
