Atoms & Molecules: Extracting Iron From Cereal

Introduction to Atoms and Molecules

Welcome, young scientists! Today we're going to dive into the fascinating world of atoms and molecules by doing a cool experiment where we'll actually extract real iron atoms from breakfast cereal!

What Are Atoms?

Atoms are the basic building blocks of all matter. They're extremely tiny - more than 10,000 times smaller than the width of a human hair! Even with a regular classroom microscope, we can't see atoms. Scientists need special equipment like a Scanning Tunneling Microscope to view individual atoms.

Parts of an Atom:

  • Protons: Positively charged particles located in the nucleus (center) of the atom
  • Neutrons: Particles with no charge, also located in the nucleus
  • Electrons: Negatively charged particles that orbit around the nucleus at tremendous speed

Most of an atom is actually empty space! The electrons orbit very far away from the nucleus, leaving a lot of empty space in between.

Fun Fact: In 1908, scientist Ernest Rutherford discovered that most of an atom is empty space by shooting radiation through gold foil. Most of the radiation passed straight through, which wouldn't happen if atoms were solid all the way through!

What Are Elements?

Elements are pure substances made of only one type of atom. The Periodic Table of Elements lists all the elements we know about.

Each element has a specific number of protons, neutrons, and electrons. The atomic number tells us how many protons and electrons an element has.

One of these elements is iron, which has the symbol Fe. Iron is a metal that has 26 protons and 26 electrons.

What Are Molecules?

When atoms combine, they form molecules. For example, water (H2O) is a molecule made of 2 hydrogen atoms bonded to 1 oxygen atom.

Some molecules are very simple, like water. Others are much more complex, like hemoglobin (the molecule in your blood that carries oxygen).

The Iron in Our Food and Bodies

Iron isn't just a metal we use to make things - it's also an essential element that our bodies need! Iron helps our blood carry oxygen to all parts of our body. Without enough iron, we can become tired and weak.

Many foods naturally contain iron, including beans, spinach, and certain cereals. Food companies often add extra iron to cereals to make them more nutritious - this is called "fortifying" the cereal.

Amazing Fact: The iron added to cereal is the same element (Fe) that's used to make steel! It's just in a form that our bodies can process.

Let's Extract Iron From Cereal!

Now for the exciting part - we're going to use a simple experiment to extract actual iron particles from breakfast cereal!

Materials Needed:

  • 1 box of Total cereal (or another iron-fortified cereal)
  • 1 strong Ziploc bag
  • 1 cup of warm water
  • 1 strong neodymium magnet

Instructions:

  1. Pour Total cereal into the bag until it is almost full.
  2. Zip the bag and crush the cereal with your hands.
  3. Fill the bag halfway with warm water, close it and mix it for 10 seconds.
  4. Let the bag sit for 1 hour to soak.
  5. Mix the bag again for 1 minute while mushing the cereal.
  6. Place the magnet under the bag and swirl the bag to collect the iron.

What's Happening in This Experiment?

In this experiment, we're using the properties of magnetism to separate iron from the rest of the cereal. When we crush the cereal and mix it with water, we're breaking down the cereal to release the tiny iron particles. Then we use a magnet to attract these iron particles because iron is magnetic!

The Science Behind It:

The iron in cereal is added as tiny iron filings - actual small pieces of elemental iron. These are the same iron atoms that would be found in an iron nail, just in much smaller pieces. Your body can absorb this iron and use it to make hemoglobin, a molecule that carries oxygen in your blood.

The magnet attracts the iron because iron is ferromagnetic - it's attracted to magnetic fields. This is the same reason magnets stick to your refrigerator!

Connecting Atoms to Real Life

This experiment shows us that atoms are real things that exist in our everyday lives - even in our food! The iron atoms in the cereal are the exact same kind of atoms that make up iron nails or steel beams, just in a form our bodies can use.

Fun Fact: An adult human body contains about 4 grams of iron - enough to make a small nail!

Careers in Chemistry

Scientists who study atoms and molecules work in the field of chemistry. Chemists create new molecules that can be used as medicines. Biochemists study large molecules like proteins and DNA that exist in living things.

For example, biochemists developed ways to produce insulin molecules to help people with diabetes. Without this important work, many people would be very sick!

Check Your Understanding

1. What are the three particles that make up an atom?
Protons, neutrons, and electrons. Protons have a positive charge, neutrons have no charge, and electrons have a negative charge.
2. Why does the magnet attract the iron in the cereal?
The magnet attracts the iron because iron is ferromagnetic, meaning it's attracted to magnetic fields. The iron in the cereal is actual elemental iron (Fe), which has the same magnetic properties as iron in nails or other metal objects.
3. Why is iron important for our bodies?
Iron is essential for our bodies because it helps our blood carry oxygen. Iron is a component of hemoglobin, which is the molecule in our blood that transports oxygen from our lungs to all parts of our body.
4. How can you tell elements apart from each other?
Elements can be identified by their atomic number, which is the number of protons they have. Each element has a unique number of protons. For example, iron (Fe) always has 26 protons, while carbon (C) always has 6 protons.
5. What is the difference between an atom and a molecule?

Conclusion

Today we've learned about atoms, the building blocks of all matter, and molecules, which are combinations of atoms. We've seen that elements like iron exist not just in metal objects but also in our food and bodies. Through our experiment, we've connected the abstract concept of atoms to something we can actually observe in the real world!

The next time you eat your breakfast cereal, remember - you're not just eating food, you're consuming atoms and molecules that your body will use to keep you healthy and strong!
